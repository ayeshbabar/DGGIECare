Introduction

It is critical for your dog's general wellbeing and health to feed them a suitable amount of well-balanced food and provide them enough exercise. (Llera and Yuill n.d.). Just like a regular workout and healthy diet is important for all human beings, the same rule applies to dogs. All dogs benefit from exercise because it keeps them mentally stimulated and active, which can help them live longer and minimise the danger of obesity (Bruke 2019). A sick dog will not only effect the health of the dog itself, but also effect the health amd emotional stability of the owner caring for the dog. Study shows that people who care for sick pets experience higher levels of depression, anxiety, and caregiver stress (Abrams 2017). Below, we will share some tips and tricks about a healthy dog diet and exercise routines to help keep your dog healthy, which will lead to a happy life for both you and your dog. So, let's get scrolling!

Dog Exercise

Puppies will grow up into adult dogs at different times, depending on the breed of the dog. A larger breed will take longer time to mature (i.e., around 15 months), while a smaller breed will mature faster (i.e., 9 months) (Pedigree n.d.). It is best to consult your regular vet to find out which category (puppy, adult, or senior) your dog falls under. Or you can use the following: Puppy Age Calculator.

Puppy Dog

The amount of exercise a puppy needs is much less compared to that required for a grown dog. Although puppies are furballs of energy, they do not require too much effort, and a ten minute walk daily is enough for them. Each month, five more minutes should be added to their daily walk-time. For example, a two months old puppy walking ten minutes daily should walk for fifteen minutes daily at the age of three months, and so on. The importance of exercising your dog is great, but keep in mind, that they should never be forced to move more than required. Forced exercise includes fast running, or bicycling with your puppy's leash in hand. This can lead to injuries that may have a life-time effect on them. Adult Dog

For adult dogs, the intensity of the workout depends on the breed (i.e, are they a large breed, or a small one?). Larger breeds, if fit, have plenty of stamina. The best type of exercise for fit large breeds is hiking, especially if you have a Husky or a Weimaraner. However, some large breeds, like Greyhounds, for example, require less exercise compared to other larger breeds. They like to use up thier energy in small spurts so it is good to allow them to run for twenty minutes and then walk for the next twenty. You can also give your large adult dog extra training to cure their boredom üòÄ.
Moving on to medium sized breeds, they too have a nice stamina. These breeds enjoy long walks, jogging/running, swimming, and hiking, etc. They are very easy to train, and enjoy playtime with their owners, such as fetching a toy or playing tug-of-war.
Smaller breeds do not require as much exercise as larger breeds. They can be quite active though. Just like lager and medium sized breeds, these smaller breeds also love playtime! They do well with just an hour of exercise daily and tend to get bored with the same old workout routines, so make sure to use some variations üòâ. You can do a combination of walking, training, puzzles and playtime to keep them happy. (psda n.d.) For more information about how to exercise your adult dog, please visit this link.

Senior Dog

Because of their movement constraints, older dogs' physical and emotional wellbeing is often overlooked. Give your senior dog constant exercise throughout their lives to prepare them up for the senior age. This will ensure that they live as long as possible in good health. Think about Physical Therapy. This can help your senior dog just they way it helps humans. There are many types therapies that you can choose for your dog, such as massage therapy, and water therapy etc.Low-impact activities should be chosen. Senior dogs require more relaxation than stimulation at this time of their lives. Instead of running, go for walks. Go for a swim if you have access to a pool or water. It's a fun exercise that's easy for their fragile bodies. Exercise should be broken up. Senior dogs require at least thirty minutes of exercise daily. Provide shorter walks and opportunities for playing throughout the day. Take a look at your dog's weight. Consider a diet adjustment or more consistent exercise for your senior dog if he or she is overweight. Maintaining a healthy weight will help you avoid as much physical stress as possible. Dog Diet

It's vital to remember that each dog is unique, and what works for your neighbor's dog may be entirely ineffective for yours. When it comes to your dog's regular nutrition, it's crucial to get advice from your veterinarian (‚ÄòWhat Should I Feed My Dog?‚Äô n.d.). However, there is some advice that we can provide you with, but please make sure to check with your vet before giving your dog something new, or if your dog is reacting to some food.
Domestic dogs are carnivores who consume some omnivore foods. The diet that you provide your dog with should be balanced and of high quality, appropriate to their stage of life (puppy, adult, pregnant, senior). The amaount of food that your furry friend requires is based on their size and breed. An adult dog, especially Boxers and German Shepherds should only be fed twice a day to prevent their bellies from swelling, as this can be fatal to them. Provide your dogs with fresh water to drink throughout the day, and do not give them milk as it can upset their stomach.
Raw meat and bones are not suggested because they can break teeth and cause intestinal obstructions. Raw meet also carries bacteria that an be harmful for both humans and dogs. Fish (such as tinned sardines, tuna, or salmon) can be given to dogs as a treat on occasions, but it should not be provided on a regular basis. Avoid fish bones and go for spring water-canned fish instead than oil or brine-canned fish.
A modest amount of prepared vegetables, such as pumpkin or carrots, can also be given to dogs. You might alternatively serve a modest amount of plain cooked pasta or rice. Prepared meat, such as boiling chicken or lamb, can also be served, but no cooked bones, onions/onion sauces, or other poisonous elements should be included. Here is a list of poisonous foods for dogs:

  • Alcohol
  • Onions
  • Onion powder
  • Garlic
  • Chocolate
  • Coffee or caffeine products
  • Mouldy foods, spoiled foods or compost
  • Avocado
  • Corncobs
  • Cooked bones
  • Salt
  • Roughly-cut vegetables
  • Bread dough
  • Yeast dough
  • Grapes, raisins, and sultanas (including in Christmas cakes etc)
  • Currants
  • Nuts (including macadamia nuts)
  • Fruit stones or ‚Äòpits‚Äô (e.g. mango seeds, apricot stones, avocado stones)
  • Mushrooms
  • Fruit seeds
  • Green unripe tomatoes
  • Small pieces of raw bone
  • Fatty trimmings/fatty foods
